Most founder stories start with "I quit my job." Mine doesn't.

I'm building DuoBrain on nights and weekends, while holding down a full-time product design job. No runway. No cofounder. No "I'll give myself 12 months to make it work."

And I think that's exactly the right way to do it.

## The lie about quitting

There's a story in startup culture that says you have to burn the boats. Quit. Go all in. Anything less means you don't believe in it.

Here's what that story misses: the people selling you that narrative are mostly the ones who *already had* savings, or rich parents, or a first exit, or a spouse with a salary. Burning the boats is a luxury good dressed up as a virtue.

I moved countries. I went through a marriage breakdown. I burned out. I'm not in a position to gamble a year of stability on a maybe.

So I build in the cracks. Mornings before work. Evenings after. Weekends when I'm not recovering from the week.

## What the constraint actually gives you

The part nobody talks about: working under constraint makes you brutal about priorities.

- I can't build every feature I want. So I only build the ones that move the one number that matters.
- I can't do every marketing experiment. So I only do the ones with a clear hypothesis.
- I can't afford vanity metrics. Followers don't matter. Downloads do.

Every hour is expensive, so every hour gets a job.

## The thing I'm actually afraid of

Not failure. Failure I can handle — I've failed before and I'll fail again. What I'm afraid of is building something nobody wanted and realizing it too late to change direction.

That's why DuoBrain is in "signal and feedback" mode right now, not growth mode. I'm not trying to get 10,000 users. I'm trying to get 100 users who will tell me the truth.

If you're one of them, I want to hear from you.
